Parking occupancy from a webcam · approach & findings · Jul 2026

Count what you can.
Add what you missed.

Estimating how many cars are in a parking lot from a single webcam frame is deceptively hard — distant cars shrink to 15 pixels and vanish. After a ladder of experiments across four cameras and six methods, one approach stands out: let a cheap object detector count what it can, then let a cheap vision model add what it missed. Consistent, verifiable, and no frontier model required.

How we got here — the ladder

Every rung was tested on the Brighton lot, where a careful human count gives us ground truth: 100 vehicles.

1 · Detect & count

62 / 100

One pass of a small segmentation model (YOLOv8-nano). It finds the obvious near cars and misses the packed far rows — nearly 40 of them.

2 · Tile the image (SAHI)

67 / 100

Slice into overlapping crops so far cars look big, detect each, merge. Helps on packed lots — but a small model still can't resolve everything.

3 · Ask a vision model, cold

62–130

Hand the raw photo to four models. They range wildly and none is reliable: the cheap ones undershoot (Gemma 62, Qwen 72, Haiku 87) and Sonnet, zooming into crops on its own, overshoots at 130.

4 · Show the model the detections

52–98

Paint the detector's guesses on the image and let the model decide. It anchors to the detector — and when the detector undercounts, that drags the models toward a low number. A trap.

5 · Floor + additions ✓

90–99

Give the model a high-recall detector count (84, from the big model + tiling) framed as a floor — “these are real, now add what's missing.” Every model lands 90 / 94 / 95 / 99 against a true 100 — the cheap ones lifted up, and Sonnet's overcount pulled back down. Tight, accurate, and the same answer regardless of price. This is the approach we're locking in.

The approach — “Floor + additions”

Step 1 · deterministic

Build a trustworthy floor

Run a high-recall detector — YOLOv8x-seg with SAHI tiling — to mark every vehicle it's confident about. On Brighton that's 84, painted as red masks with a green dot at each. Free, offline, repeatable.

CV · YOLOv8x-seg + tiling → floor = 84
Step 2 · cheap model

Add the misses

Hand that overlay to a small vision model with one instruction: “84 is a floor — the marked cars are real, but distant and hidden ones are missing. Add them.” The model only supplements; it never re-judges down.

VLM → total = floor + missed
Why it works. The plain hybrid failed because a weak detector's markers pulled the model toward a low number. Two changes flip it: a high-recall floor (84, not 62) so the anchor points up, and a “supplement, don't re-judge” framing so the model's job is only to add. The result is consistent counts from cheap, swappable models — the accuracy ceiling is now the detector's recall, not the model. Push recall past 84 and the totals rise with it.
The floor is a stabilizer, not just a lift. Against a true 100, it pulls the cheap models up (Gemma 62→95) and reins Sonnet's overcount back down (130→94). Everyone converges near floor + a small margin, so the one number that matters is the floor's recall — here 84 of 100. Get the floor right and the whole cluster is right; the model you pair it with barely matters.

The models — pros & cons

YOLOv8 (CV)

the detector / the floor

  • Free, offline, deterministic
  • Gives an auditable, per-car floor
  • Recall scales with model size + tiling
  • Undercounts distant / packed rows
  • No sense of “how full”

Role: the floor. Always runs first.

Claude Sonnet 5

the careful one

  • Self-directs: zooms, crops, cross-checks
  • Critiques bad detections instead of trusting them
  • Best judgment on ambiguous, packed clusters
  • Overcounts cold (130 vs 100)
  • Slowest and priciest by far

Role: the floor corrects its overcount (130→94) — pair it, don't run it raw.

Claude Haiku 4.5

the fast one

  • Fast and cheap
  • Stable, low-variance
  • Coarse on fullness (pins ~75%)
  • Conservative — undercounts, adds few misses

Role: good “empty vs busy”, weak on exact counts.

Gemma 4 · Ollama

open · local or cloud

  • Open weights, runs on your own hardware
  • Biggest gain from the floor (62→95)
  • Weakest counting the photo cold

Role: strong once floor-assisted; keeps data in-house.

Qwen 3.5 · Ollama

open · local or cloud

  • Open weights; strong with assist (72→99)
  • Counts aggressively — good for “add misses”
  • High variance; cloud endpoint was flaky

Role: capable floor-adder; watch the variance.

The other half — “% full”

Raw counts answer “how many.” Just as useful for operations is “how full,” and it's a genuinely different question — three, actually:

Count ÷ capacity

Cars over how many the lot holds. Objective, if you know both — and capacity is usually a guess.

Surface coverage

Fraction of ground under metal, perspective-corrected. Needs no capacity; on clean lots it tracked the eye.

Holistic judgment

What the vision models do — fast, but they read high, and Haiku pins ~75% for anything busy.

What % full needs next: calibration frames

We can compute all three today, but we can't tune the mapping until we have anchors — a capture of each lot at 0% (empty) and 100% (full). Those two frames pin the surface-coverage scale and calibrate the models' high bias. Capturing an empty overnight frame and a peak-day full frame per camera is the next data-collection step; the metric stays on the roadmap.

Honest limits

Ground truth is a single human count on one Brighton frame (100) — and even that is fuzzy; a packed lot is genuinely hard to count exactly. Every number is one point-in-time read, not an average. Lot regions and capacities are rough. Floor+additions lands within ~10 of 100, but that accuracy rides entirely on the detector's recall (84 here) — a weaker floor means a weaker cluster. And “% full” stays uncalibrated until we capture the 0% and 100% anchors.
